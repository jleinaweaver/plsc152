Reading Notes
Intro (172-173)
- "Feminist scholars are beginning to study the language used in UN-related documents and are concluding that the way women are defined, as civilians, victims, vulnerable and in association with children, is not only essentialist but undermines women’s agency" (172).
- Charli Carpenter: ‘[t]hrough their association with children, women, but not men, have been constructed as possessing the attributes associated with a claim to immunity: innocence and vulnerability’.
- The Argument: "As this article attempts to show, the masculinist language of UN peacekeeping documents has been translated into operational practice that perpetuates a vision of gender roles that reinforces inequalities and prevents progress on gender mainstreaming" (173).
- Method: "analyses the language of ten UN Secretary-General reports on peacekeeping operations, with the aim of identifying a pattern that confirms the findings of academic research on the victimization of women" (173).

Reports of the Secretary-General on Peacekeeping Operations (173-175)
- Cases: Reports of the Secretary-General on Peacekeeping Operations in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) (MONUC), Haiti (MINUSTAH), Liberia (UNMIL), Timor-Leste (UNMIT), Darfur (UNMAID), Sudan (UNMIS), Nepal (UNMIN), Chad (MINURCAT), Côte d’Ivoire (ONUCI) and Kosovo (UNMIK).
- "On the absence of gender disaggregated statistics Cynthia Enloe observes that ‘[t]here’s an enormous incentive not to gender-disaggregate data. It makes inequities invisible. Once you make inequities visible, you are also likely to make visible the power dynamics that create those inequities. There is a lot of incentive to be uncurious [sic]. Incuriosity is a political act’ (174).
- "Peacekeeping operations have set up a criterion of success according to a strong masculine norm of reference. ...the ‘masculine norm’ in peacekeeping is defined here as a complex of behaviours and attitudes that privilege physical toughness, heterosexual macho bravura, the denigration of women and femininity, an exclusionary focus on issues affecting men and, in a Foucauldian sense, disciplinary approaches to multifaceted problems. The institutionalization of this norm means that decision-makers are unwilling to consider criteria that would reveal that many issues at stake in a host country specifically affect the realities of women" (174).
- "By continuing to report about ‘people’, ‘communities’, ‘groups’, ‘populations’, male decision-makers do not differentiate women and men’s needs in peacekeeping situations" (174).
- "Notwithstanding a dominant male norm of reference in place within each of the missions surveyed, most reports include a paragraph on gender (with the exception of Kosovo, Darfur, East-Timor and Nepal), which mainly describes activities revolving around resolution 1325. However, the existence of a separate section on gender very often resulted in gender not being mainstreamed throughout the main body of the reports" (175).
- Nepal example "does not contain a single word on gender-related issues" because the UNMIN Gender Section was closed in 2008. "It reveals that in Nepal, as in other peacekeeping operations, no one is accountable on gender issues except the Gender Office/Section of the mission" (175).
- "One may wonder why women are always treated as a separate variable based on sex, just as there are variables based on age, disability or ethnicity. The French anthropologist Françoise Héritier explains that each time we use sex as a sociological variable, we implicitly accept the fact that women belong to a separate category, are treated like a minority and are in a position  of inferiority relative to a masculine norm of reference used to define social phenomena from their male vantage points (175).

The ‘Women and Children’ Syndrome (175-178)
- "As the feminist writer Adrienne Rich emphasizes... '...In the eyes of society, the mother is the child’s environment’" (176).
- "Charli Carpenter comments, ‘Women have traditionally been associated with child-rearing, and the special protections that have accrued to women under international humanitarian law have historically addressed only their specific needs as mothers rather than the vulnerabilities they face as a result of gender hierarchies prevalent in society before and during armed conflict'" (176).
- "Chief among the challenges in promoting a different image of women, beyond their status as victims of war, is the lack of accurate data" (176).
- "Yet, as the 2005 Human Security Report notes, ‘[t]he increased participation of women in government military forces, rebel groups and even terrorist organizations also serves to remind us that depicting women simply as passive victims of political violence can be profoundly misleading’" (176-177).
- "The stereotyping of women appears to be used to keep them away from the peace negotiation table on the grounds that they did not participate in the fighting" (177).
- "This short-sightedness prevents an all-inclusive approach that would comprehend the different needs and expectations of women and men in the reconstruction of a post-conflict society" (177).
- "Not all women are civilians and not all men are combatants" (177). 
- Women in combat zones can face a "double burden": The Serbian case shows women being targeted as an environment with assault to harm the males who could not protect "their women" AND women are expected to help the family cope if the men are killed.
- "As war participants – as combatants for example – women would be invited to negotiations to discuss issues at stake for the reconstruction of the post-war society. They would be consulted by international personnel about their needs and expectations and they would be considered serious interlocutors. Instead, UN peacekeepers and humanitarian workers often seem to look out for the male holders of formal power at the local level, the so-called elders, mayors or gang leaders – overlooking women’s organizations that might be considered a major source of information, especially in the field of security" (178).
- "Women are not a homogeneous group who all have the same needs. By putting all women in the same basket of vulnerable individuals, one asserts that they are all victims, civilians and mothers with children, irrespective of their individual background, situation and capacity. UN actions then become solely about protection and "These UN practices have far-reaching implications for the women themselves who are trapped in the straitjacket of victimization" (178).

Patriarchy as a Language (178-181)
- Cynthia Enloe: "It is not men-on-top that makes something patriarchal. It’s men who are recognized and claim a certain form of masculinity, for the sake of being more valued, more “serious”, and “the protector of/and controllers of those people who are less masculine” that makes any organization, any community, any society patriarchal’" (179).
- Marilyn French: "Men treat women as marginal to the real business of life, not the essential  maintainers they are. Even when feminists oblige men to hear them, politicians treat them as a “special interest group” – as if their concerns affected a small fraction of the population, not all women" (179).
- "Angela Raven-Roberts observes that ‘[i]n emergency contexts there is “no time” to do gender work, as what is needed is rapid action, lifesaving food, and material distribution. Performing nuanced analysis and targeting change is too cumbersome, complex, and time consuming, indeed downright harmful to the “real work” of saving lives" (179).
- "Meredeth Turshen argues, in the cases of Eritrea or Mozambique, that ‘When the war stops, when things go back to normal, people go back to what they know, to what they are comfortable with, to what they identify with, which is the traditional society that often if not advantageous to women’" (180).
- "Research has revealed that armed conflict is a time when patriarchal barriers break down, thus allowing a redefinition of gender roles: ‘Conflict, war and shifting social orders provide women with opportunities to break out of stereotypes and stifling societal patterns. . . . If women seize these opportunities, transformation is possible'" (180).
- "Chinkin illustrates this change: ‘In many instances, conflict will have empowered women and opened up new opportunities that did not previously exist. The goal should rather be societal transformation – not restored dependence and subordination'" (180).
- "In UN-related documentation, language associates women with the promotion of peace and the preservation of social order, thus defining them in an essentialist role as so-called ‘natural’ peacemakers because of their participation in the private realm as mothers, caretakers, caregivers, mediators..." (180).
- "Thus when women are not defined as helpless victims in need of protection, they are depicted as being more peaceful than men and their contribution to peace-making is hailed as essential and, as such, encouraged" (181).

The Gendered Silences of Resolution 1325 (181-183)
- "There are no accountability mechanisms for resolution 1325 that keep track of how the top management of peacekeeping operations implements the resolution in its daily work" (181).
- "In May 2008, the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) conducted an internet study" that showed female officials much more likely to 'advocate for
the increased role of women in peace and security operations'" but there were only 6 women out of the 66 "managers occupying top management positions in UN peacebuilding. ... The UNHCR’s study clearly shows that ‘[t]here appears to be a direct correlation between a failure to promote women to high levels in the UN peacebuilding system and a lack of advocacy for women’s rights in peacebuilding’" (181-182).
- "Why does it remain so difficult to appoint more women to the decisionmaking levels of peace operations? There is an obvious lack of political will and commitment within the UN hierarchy, which, permeated as it is with highly masculinized attitudes, exhibits a persistent resistance to change and power-sharing" (182).
- "As Hilary Charlesworth argues, ‘[t]he imbalance in men’s representation in national and international government structures allows male life experiences to be regarded as a general, rather than a gendered, category. By contrast, “women’s concerns” are regarded as a distinct and limited category, relegated to a specialized and marginalized sphere and regulated, if at all, in a weaker way'" (182).
- "Even in 2007, there is “underlying resistance” to women’s leadership in military and diplomatic circles. They are still seen as not having the political skills and the diplomatic gravitas to head large-scale civil – military missions'" (182).
- "In all too many cases it would appear that reference to resolution 1325 has been used by UN decision-makers as a proxy for appearing to be concerned with gender-related issues in the country of mission without actually committing to its implementation" (182).
- "Resolution 1325 has provided top-ranking officials in peace operations with an opportunity to pay lip service to gender issues, in much the same vein that they respond to International Women’s Day on 8 March" (183).

Conclusion (184)
- "Resolution 1325, which has been translated into different languages, has enabled women’s organizations to lobby their governments and to advocate for women’s rights" (184).
- "Nevertheless the language of resolution 1325, the constant association and interchangeability of ‘gender’ with ‘women’, weakens the scope of the UN’s ability to de-gender peace and security" (184).
- "‘One of the reasons gender has become a safe idea is that the manner in which it has been used within UN understandings of peace and security issues has transformed it from a radical perspective to a problem-solving tool, which does not challenge prevailing practices in armed  conflict, peace and security’" (184).
- "The problem is that the UN continues to function as a system that is defined according to well-grounded masculinist norms. These norms are the barometer that define who is included and excluded from the corridors of power. In this context, resolution 1325 may appear to be an aberration, its adoption was hailed as a transformatory triumph but in reality it has been used as a means of coopting gender dynamics in order to preserve the existing gender status quo. Thus relations of inequality and the imbalance of power between women and men within the UN system remain uncontested, despite the existence of resolution 1325" (184).
