A Comparative Case Study


“Comparative case studies involve the analysis and synthesis of the similarities, differences and patterns across two or more cases that share a common focus or goal in a way that produces knowledge that is easier to generalize about causal questions – how and why particular programmes or policies work or fail to work” (Goodrick 2014).

In other words, comparing all of your cases allows us to:

  1. Broaden our descriptive knowledge of the world,

  2. Identify puzzles that need some explanation (generates new research projects), and

  3. Develop generalizations about how certain mechanisms or processes might work.


I’m hoping we can do some of all three of those things today.

The question might not be so cut and dry.


Here we see the Fragile States Index.

  • Some have demonstrated that states with increasing levels of human rights violations raise their risk of failure (e.g. governmental collapse).

  • And state failures tend not to stay isolated within their borders, e.g. one failed state tends to destabilize those around them.

  • Therefore, preventing small conflicts from becoming wider wars may require earlier interference in states when human rights violations increase.


Does this change how you answer the question about American involvement in conflicts overseas? Why or why not?
